Effective Note-Taking — Glossary

Key terms used across all lessons in this workspace.

Transcription Trap
The mistake of copying information verbatim into notes without processing it. Feels productive but produces no real understanding or retention.
E.g. copying a quote word-for-word from a textbook without adding any interpretation or connection.
Cornell Method
A structured note-taking format with three zones: a Notes column (your understanding in your own words), a Cue column (questions and keywords added after), and a Summary section at the bottom.
Used widely in universities for its forced processing at each stage.
Cue Column
The left margin of a Cornell note — filled in after note-taking with questions, keywords, or prompts designed to trigger retrieval of the corresponding note content.
Elaborative Interrogation
A study technique where you ask "why" and "how" about every new idea. Writing the answers forces deeper processing than simply reading or re-reading.
E.g. "Why does this happen? How does this connect to what I already know?"
Zettelkasten
A note-taking method developed by Niklas Luhmann: each note is atomic (one idea only), written in your own words, and linked explicitly to other notes by idea — not by topic or date.
Atomic Note
A note that captures exactly one idea — no more. The unit used in Zettelkasten. Makes notes easier to link, retrieve, and reuse later.
Progressive Summarisation
A technique by Tiago Forte: highlight the most important passage, then bold the most important sentence within that, then create a short executive summary. Notes are distilled in layers over multiple passes.
Retrieval Trigger
A keyword, question, or visual cue in a note designed to prompt memory of the full concept — used in the Cornell Cue column and flashcards.
Capture vs. Process
Two distinct phases of note-taking. Capture is getting raw input down quickly (speed matters). Process is transforming that raw input into understanding (quality matters). Conflating them produces bad notes.
PKM (Personal Knowledge Management)
The broader practice of organising and connecting your own knowledge over time — using tools like Obsidian, Notion, or Roam. Note-taking is one component of a PKM system.
