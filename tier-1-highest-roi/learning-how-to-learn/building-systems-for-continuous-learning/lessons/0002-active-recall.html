Active Recall

The single most effective study technique known to science — and almost no one uses it correctly.

What you'll learn

  • What active recall is and why it beats rereading
  • The testing effect — why self-testing builds stronger memories
  • Three practical methods to use active recall starting today
  • A hands-on exercise to practice the technique right now

The passive learning trap

Most people learn like this: they read something, feel like they understand it, maybe highlight a few sentences, and move on. Then they wonder why it doesn't stick.

This is passive learning. And it's deceptively comfortable — re-reading familiar material feels productive. Your brain recognizes the words and generates a pleasant feeling of fluency. But familiarity is not the same as memory. You're not building the neural pathways that would let you retrieve and use this knowledge when it matters. (Make It Stick, Shortform)

Passive learning Active recall
Reading your notes again low retention Closing your notes, asking "what do I remember?" high retention
Watching a video twice low retention Pausing the video and summarising it from memory high retention
Re-reading highlighted text low retention Writing down what you remember before looking high retention
Copying notes from a board low retention Closing the board, recalling what was on it high retention

The testing effect

The science behind active recall is called the testing effect (also called the retrieval practice effect). It's one of the most robust and replicated findings in cognitive psychology.

The landmark study: In 2006, Roediger and Karpicke gave students a passage to study. One group reread it four times. Another group read it once, then tested themselves three times. A week later, they were tested. The testing group remembered 50% more. (Recallify — Evidence Review)

Why this works Every time you retrieve a memory, you don't just passively "access" it — you rebuild it. The act of reconstruction strengthens the neural connections involved. Memories retrieved are harder to forget than memories merely re-exposed.

The effort matters too. Struggling to remember something — even if you don't fully succeed — produces stronger memories than looking up the answer immediately. The difficulty is the point. (Make It Stick)

Three methods you can use today

1. The Brain Dump

Best for: after reading, after a lesson, after a meeting

After learning something, close all your materials and write (or speak aloud) everything you can remember. No peeking. Then check what you missed. This is the simplest and most immediate form of active recall — and it works on anything.

2. Flashcards (done right)

Best for: facts, definitions, vocabulary, formulas

Write a question on one side, the answer on the other. Crucially: see the question, generate the answer from memory before flipping. The generation attempt is what creates the memory. Don't flip immediately when you feel uncertain — struggle first.

3. The Feynman Technique

Best for: concepts, processes, anything you need to deeply understand

After learning something, explain it in simple language as if teaching a 12-year-old. The gaps in your explanation reveal the gaps in your understanding. Where your explanation breaks down — that's exactly what you need to study more. (Scott H. Young — Ultralearning)

The rule Every time you finish learning something — a video, a chapter, a lesson — your first action should be to close the material and try to recall what you learned before looking again. Not after. Before.

Connecting back to Lesson 1

Remember the forgetting curve from Lesson 1? Active recall is the mechanism that resets it. Each retrieval attempt flattens the curve — your brain receives the signal: "This information is needed. Keep it."

This is why spaced repetition (which we'll cover in the next lesson) uses active recall as its testing method. It's not just about when you review — it's about how. Testing yourself, not rereading, is what makes the reviews count.
