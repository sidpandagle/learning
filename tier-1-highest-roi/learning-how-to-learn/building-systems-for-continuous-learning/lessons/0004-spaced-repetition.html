Spaced Repetition

You now know why you forget (Lesson 1) and how to retrieve (Lesson 2). This lesson gives you the when — a scheduling system that makes your review time five times more efficient.

What you'll learn

  • What spaced repetition is and why the timing of review matters
  • How a spaced repetition system (SRS) schedules your reviews automatically
  • The rules for writing cards that actually work — and the mistakes to avoid
  • How to start: the manual option and the Anki option

The problem with random review

In Lesson 1 you learned that memory decays on a curve — and that each successful review resets that curve, letting it decay more slowly next time. In Lesson 2 you learned to use active recall rather than rereading.

But there's still a timing problem. If you review too soon after learning something (when you still remember it clearly), you waste effort — the memory was never at risk of being lost. If you review too late (after you've already forgotten), the review feels like relearning from scratch, which is slow.

The optimal moment to review is just before you forget. Review there, and you get the maximum memory boost with the minimum effort. (Recallify — Evidence for Active Recall and Spaced Repetition)

How spaced repetition works

Spaced repetition is a system that reviews each piece of knowledge at increasing intervals — timed to hit just before it would be forgotten.

Example: learning a new concept

Day 1
Learn
Day 2
Review #1
→ interval resets to ~4 days
Day 6
Review #2
→ interval grows to ~10 days
Day 16
Review #3
→ interval grows to ~3 weeks
Day 37
Review #4
→ interval now ~2 months

Each time you successfully recall something, the system extends the interval. Items you find hard get reviewed more often. Items you find easy get pushed further into the future. The result: you spend your time exactly where it's needed.

The research result Studies consistently show spaced repetition produces roughly the same long-term retention as massed practice (cramming) — but in 40–60% less total study time. The same outcome, dramatically less effort. (ERIC — Spaced Repetition in STEM)

What goes on a flashcard — and what doesn't

Spaced repetition lives or dies on card quality. A poorly written card wastes reviews. A well-written card makes learning fast and durable.

The core rule: one question, one answer, one idea. If a card covers two facts, you can't separate what you know from what you don't — so the system can't schedule it accurately.

Bad card
Q: What is spaced repetition and how does it work and why is it better than cramming?
A: It's a review system that spaces out reviews over increasing intervals, exploiting the spacing effect, and studies show it produces the same retention with 40–60% less time...
Too broad. One card, three concepts. If you forget one part, you can't score it honestly.
Good card
Q: What is the spacing effect?
A: Reviewing the same material spread across multiple sessions produces stronger long-term retention than reviewing it all at once.
One idea. Short answer. Easy to judge right or wrong. Fast to review.
The most common mistake Don't copy text from a book onto a card. Rewrite it in your own words first. If you can't explain it without looking, you don't understand it well enough to make a card for it yet — go back and learn it properly, then make the card.

A few more card rules that pay off immediately:

  • ✓ Make it personal. "What does spaced repetition mean for me?" beats "Define spaced repetition." Your brain retains things that feel relevant.
  • ✓ Use cloze deletions for facts. "The spacing effect shows reviews spread over time produce [___] retention than cramming." Forces active recall without needing a full Q/A format.
  • ✓ Keep answers short. If your answer is more than two sentences, split the card.
  • ✗ Don't add cards for things you already know well. Cards are expensive — they eat future review time. Only add cards for things you genuinely need to retain.

Two ways to start

You don't need an app to use spaced repetition. There are two practical options depending on how you want to start.

Option A — Paper (zero friction to start)

Write questions on one side of index cards, answers on the other. Sort your deck into three piles: Review today, Review tomorrow, Review next week. Each correct recall moves a card to a later pile. Each wrong recall moves it back to "today."

Good if you want to try spaced repetition before committing to an app.

Option B — Anki (free, algorithm-driven)

Anki is the most widely used SRS tool. It runs the SM-2 algorithm behind the scenes — you just rate your recall after each card, and it handles all the scheduling. Available on desktop (free) and mobile. You can add images, audio, and formatting.

Best long-term option. Steeper setup, but you'll stop thinking about the scheduling entirely.

Recommendation Start with paper for one week. Make 10–15 cards on something you're actually trying to learn. Run one review session per day using the three-pile method. After a week you'll understand exactly what Anki automates — and you'll know if you want it.

Fitting spaced repetition into your habit

In Lesson 3 you built a habit stack using a cue → routine → reward formula. Spaced repetition is the review method that slots into that routine.

A practical daily flow: 5–10 minutes of card review in the morning (your SRS session), then one new learning session whenever it fits your day (reading, video, course). The morning review handles retention. The learning session handles acquisition. Together, nothing falls through the cracks.

This week's action Make 10 cards on a topic you're actively learning. Use paper or Anki. Review them once a day for 5 days. Notice which cards you keep getting wrong — those are the concepts you don't actually understand yet. That's valuable signal: go back and learn those concepts more deeply before adding more cards.

Ask your teacher

Good questions to continue with: "I made cards but they feel too easy — am I doing something wrong?" or "How do I organise cards across multiple subjects?" or "I keep writing cards that are too long — can you help me split them?" Your teacher adapts to what you're actually running into.
