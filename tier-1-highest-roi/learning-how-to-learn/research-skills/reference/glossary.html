Research Skills — Glossary

Key terms used across all lessons in this workspace.

SIFT
A four-step source evaluation method: Stop, Investigate the source, Find better coverage, Trace claims to origin. Developed by Mike Caulfield. Designed for fast, reliable online source evaluation.
Lateral Reading
Opening new tabs to research a source *while* on the source's page — rather than reading the source deeply first. How professional fact-checkers work. More efficient than reading everything before evaluating.
Instead of reading the full article on a site you don't know, open a tab and search the site's name first.
Primary Source
The original source of a claim: a peer-reviewed study, official government data, a direct quote, a raw dataset. The most reliable type of source — but requires knowing how to read it.
Secondary Source
A source that interprets or summarises a primary source — journalism, blog posts, textbooks. Can be high quality, but always carries the risk of distortion from the original.
Authority Borrowing
A deception pattern where a low-credibility source cites a high-credibility institution to appear legitimate — without the institution's endorsement or accurately representing their findings.
E.g. "Harvard researchers discovered..." with no link, no author name, and no actual study.
CRAAP Test
A source evaluation checklist: Currency (is it current?), Relevance (is it relevant to your question?), Authority (who wrote it?), Accuracy (is it supported by evidence?), Purpose (why was it written?).
Peer Review
The process by which academic papers are evaluated by independent experts in the same field before publication. A peer-reviewed source has been checked for methodology and claims — though not infallible.
Meta-Analysis
A study that aggregates and statistically combines results from multiple independent studies on the same question. Generally higher quality evidence than a single study.
Vague Sourcing
Language like "studies show," "experts say," or "researchers found" with no specific citation, author, or institution named. A red flag — always demand the specific source.
Confirmation Bias (in research)
The tendency to search for, favour, and remember sources that confirm what you already believe. A key threat to research quality — the best researchers actively seek out counter-evidence.
Signal-to-Noise Ratio
The proportion of reliable, relevant information vs. low-quality or irrelevant information in a search. Good research skills = maximising signal, minimising noise.
