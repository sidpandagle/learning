Note-Taking in Real Time

Meetings, videos, and talks don't wait for you to think. Here's how to capture what matters when you can't slow down.

What you'll learn

  • Why trying to capture everything in real time always fails
  • The two-pass approach: capture fast, process after
  • Exactly what to capture in meetings — and what to ignore
  • A shorthand toolkit for faster note-taking
  • The 5-minute post-meeting process that turns raw notes into useful ones

The real-time problem

Books let you pause. Meetings, videos, and live talks don't. Information arrives at a fixed pace — often faster than you can write, understand, and process simultaneously. Most people respond to this by trying to capture everything, and end up with notes that are simultaneously too long and too thin to be useful.

The solution isn't faster writing. It's a two-pass system: capture first (fast, messy, incomplete), process after (slow, clean, deliberate).

Pass 1: Capture (during)
  • Speed over neatness
  • Fragments and shorthand OK
  • Mark unclear spots with ?
  • Don't re-read or edit yet
  • Just keep up
Pass 2: Process (after)
  • Fill in gaps while fresh
  • Add cues and connections
  • Write a 2-sentence summary
  • Extract decisions and actions
  • Delete what wasn't useful
The key insight Pass 1 and Pass 2 require completely different mental modes. Don't try to do both at once — that's the mistake that produces pages of notes you never use. Capture messily. Process deliberately. Always separate.

What to capture in meetings

Most meeting notes are bloated because people record context, conversation, and pleasantries instead of output. A meeting produces three types of valuable content — everything else is noise.

What to capture Example
Decisions
DEC: shipping Thursday, not Friday. Budget approved at £5k.
Actions (who/what/when)
ACT: [Sarah] send revised brief → [Mon]. [Me] review specs → [Wed].
Surprising statements
! "70% of support tickets are about onboarding" — hadn't realised

That's it. Context you already know, process discussion, and background information don't need to be captured — you were there. The only things that need to survive the meeting are things you'd otherwise forget or dispute later.

Use a simple prefix system: DEC: for decisions, ACT: for action items, ! for surprises. Scan for these prefixes when processing — everything unmarked can be quickly reviewed and deleted.

A shorthand toolkit

Good shorthand speeds up capture without reducing quality. These symbols are readable by any future version of you:

→leads to / causes
∴therefore
∵because
≈approximately
vs.compared to
↑↓increase / decrease
w/with
w/owithout
?unclear — follow up
!surprising / important
*return to this
→meaction for me

You don't need all of these — pick 5–6 you'll actually use and stick to them. Consistency matters more than completeness.

The 5-minute post-meeting process

The most valuable thing you can do immediately after any meeting or talk is spend 5 minutes processing your notes. Context evaporates fast — what seems obvious now will be cryptic tomorrow.

1 min

Fill gaps. Find every "?" and complete the thought while you still remember what you meant. Add context that only makes sense because you were in the room.

2 min

Extract actions and decisions. Copy all DEC: and ACT: items to wherever you track tasks — your to-do app, project doc, or calendar. Don't leave them buried in notes.

1 min

Write one summary sentence. "The main outcome of this meeting was…" If you can't write it, the meeting may not have had one — worth noting either way.

1 min

Delete the rest. Strike out everything that wasn't a decision, action, or surprise. What's left is your real note.

Your one action from this lesson In your next meeting or video, use the prefix system: mark DEC:, ACT:, and ! as you go. Don't try to write everything else. Immediately after, spend 5 minutes on the post-meeting process. Compare how much useful information you have vs. your usual approach.
Self-Check

Test what you just learned

1. What is the core idea behind the two-pass approach to real-time note-taking?

A Write notes twice — once during and once from memory afterward — to reinforce retention
B One person captures during the meeting while another processes immediately after
C Separate fast, messy capture from slow, deliberate processing — because they require different mental modes that can't be done simultaneously
D Take notes on paper first, then type them up digitally for storage
Exactly. Capture and process are cognitively incompatible in real time — you can't deeply understand something while simultaneously trying to keep up with incoming information. Separate them.
The two-pass approach separates capture from processing because they require different mental modes. Trying to do both simultaneously produces notes that are simultaneously too long (you captured too much) and too thin (you didn't process any of it).

2. In a meeting, a colleague spends 10 minutes explaining the background context of a project you've worked on for months. What should your notes contain?

A A detailed summary of the background — it might be useful to have a written record
B The most important 3–4 sentences from the background explanation
C Nothing from that section — you already know the background, and no decision, action, or surprise occurred
D A note that "background was discussed" to log that it happened
Correct. You were there, and you already knew the context. Notes should capture what you'd otherwise forget or dispute — not what you already know. Skip the background entirely.
If you already know the background and no decision, action, or surprise came from that section — skip it completely. Notes are for what you'd otherwise forget or dispute later, not a transcript of what was said.

3. Why should you do the 5-minute post-meeting process immediately after the meeting ends?

A So you can share the notes with the team before they forget what was discussed
B Because meeting context evaporates fast — what makes sense now will be cryptic tomorrow, and gaps can only be filled while the session is fresh
C Because it's easier to process notes before your next meeting starts
D So you can update your calendar with any dates that were mentioned
Yes. Context is in your working memory right after the meeting and gone within hours. Every "?" you left in the notes is answerable right now — and unanswerable tomorrow. Process immediately.
The reason to process immediately is that meeting context lives in working memory — and working memory clears fast. Every unclear note you had is answerable right now. Wait an hour and those gaps become permanent.
