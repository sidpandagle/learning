Reading Comprehension — Glossary

Key terms used across all lessons in this workspace.

Retention Gap
The difference between what you read and what you actually remember afterward. Caused primarily by passive reading — receiving words without active cognitive engagement.
Active Reading
Reading with a cognitive purpose: asking questions before you read, predicting what comes next, and pausing to retrieve and summarise what you've just read. Produces significantly higher retention than passive reading.
Passive Reading
Reading where the eyes move across words without deeper processing — no questions asked, no predictions made, no retrieval attempted. Common default mode. Retention is very low.
SQ3R
A structured reading method: Survey, Question, Read, Recite, Review. Developed by Francis P. Robinson (1941). Each step is designed to increase active engagement with the material.
Survey
The first step of SQ3R: scanning the entire piece before reading — headings, subheadings, intro, conclusion, figures. Builds a mental skeleton the details can attach to.
Takes 2–3 minutes. Done before reading a single word of main text.
Question
The second step of SQ3R: converting each heading into a genuine question. Gives your reading a purpose — you're hunting for answers, not passively absorbing words.
"The Causes of Inflation" → "What are the causes of inflation, and which is most significant?"
Recite
After reading each section: closing the material and answering your questions from memory. The retrieval attempt — even if imperfect — is where most retention is built.
Elaborative Interrogation
A reading technique: asking "why" and "how" about each new idea as you read, then answering in your own words. Proved to significantly improve retention over rereading.
Inspectional Reading
Term from Mortimer Adler's "How to Read a Book": rapid reading to understand what a book is about and whether it's worth reading carefully. Equivalent to the Survey step.
Analytical Reading
Adler's term for deep, engaged reading of a single book — identifying the structure, the central argument, and the key terms, then critically evaluating the argument.
Mental Skeleton
The structural overview of a piece built during the Survey step. Provides the framework your brain uses to organise new information as it arrives during reading.
