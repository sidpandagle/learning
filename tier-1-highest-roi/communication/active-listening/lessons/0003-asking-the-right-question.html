Asking the Right Question

After you reflect, what do you say next? The difference between a question that opens a conversation and one that quietly redirects it back to your agenda.

The reflection opened a door. Now what?

In Lesson 2, you learned to reflect before you respond — paraphrase what the speaker meant, and let that land. The reflection creates space. The speaker either confirms you understood, or corrects you.

What happens after that space is the next decision. Most people fill it with one of two things: a question, or their own point. If you use a question, the quality of that question determines whether the conversation goes deeper or gets redirected back to you.

The hidden agenda problem Not all questions are created equal. Some questions are genuinely curious — they invite the speaker to go further. Others are rhetorical — they are opinions dressed up as questions. A question that starts with "Don't you think…" or "Wouldn't it make more sense to…" is not really a question. It's your agenda wearing a question mark.

Two types of questions

Opens the conversation
"What's driving that concern?"
"How are you thinking about it?"
"What would success look like for you?"
"What's making this harder than expected?"
Invites the speaker to think out loud. You learn something you didn't already know. The direction is theirs to choose.
Redirects to your agenda
"Don't you think we should just…?"
"Have you considered doing X instead?"
"Wouldn't it be simpler to…?"
"Why didn't you just…?"
Signals your preferred answer before they've finished explaining their situation. The direction is yours, not theirs.

The difference is not about open vs. closed syntax ("what" vs. "yes/no"). It's about who the question serves. A question serves the speaker when it invites them to go further in their direction. It serves you when it steers them toward a conclusion you've already reached.

Research finding Studies on high-performing teams consistently find that psychological safety — the degree to which people feel free to speak openly — is the single strongest predictor of team effectiveness. Curiosity-driven questions are one of the fastest ways to signal that safety to another person. (Harvard Business Review, 2017)

The full loop, so far

You now have the first two moves of a complete listening sequence:

  1. Reflect — paraphrase what they meant before you respond
  2. Ask — if you don't fully understand, ask a question that opens, not one that redirects

Used together, these two moves cover most meeting situations where listening breaks down. The reflection proves you heard them. The question signals you want to understand more, not just rebut.

In practice You don't have to use both every time. If the reflection is enough — if the speaker confirms you got it — move on. Use the question when there's still something you genuinely don't understand after reflecting. "What's behind that for you?" is better than a follow-on reflection that's just guessing.

Question quality at a glance

What the speaker said Question Type
"I'm worried about the timeline." "What part of the timeline is most uncertain for you right now?" Opens
"I'm worried about the timeline." "Don't you think we have enough buffer?" Agenda
"I'm not sure this is the right approach." "What would make you more confident in it?" Opens
"I'm not sure this is the right approach." "Have you looked at the data we shared?" Agenda
"I need more time on this." "How much time are you thinking, and what's driving that?" Opens
"I need more time on this." "Can we at least move forward with part of it?" Moves on

Practice: Which question opens it?

The habit to build

Before asking a question in your next meeting, pause one beat and ask yourself: does this question give them a direction to go, or does it push them toward a conclusion I've already reached? If you can hear your own answer embedded in the question, rewrite it.

What you've learned

  • After reflecting, the next move is either "that's enough, respond" or "I still don't fully understand — ask"
  • An opening question invites the speaker to go further in their direction
  • An agenda question steers toward your preferred conclusion — even if it's phrased as curiosity
  • The tell: if your answer is embedded in the question, it's not really a question
