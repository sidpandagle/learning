Reflective Listening

One sentence before you respond. How to prove to the speaker — and to your own brain — that you actually heard them.

The technique in one line

Before you respond to anything in a meeting, you reflect back what you heard. Not repeating their words. Not agreeing with them. Just showing that you understood what they said.

This sounds simple. It is not natural. Most of the time, you'll feel an impulse to skip it — because you already know what to say. That impulse is exactly the cognitive surplus filling up with your response instead of their meaning.

Why it works Carl Rogers, who codified reflective listening in the 1950s, showed that when people feel genuinely heard, they continue sharing — and they often surface the real issue, not just the presenting one. In a meeting, what someone says first is rarely the complete picture. The reflection is what opens the rest of it. (Positive Psychology, 2024)

The formula

The reflective statement
Starter phrase + what you understood them to mean
You are not quoting them back. You are paraphrasing —
capturing the meaning, not the exact words.

Starter phrases that work in meetings:

  • "So what I'm hearing is…"
  • "It sounds like…"
  • "If I'm understanding you right…"
  • "Sounds like you're saying…"

Each of these signals: I am checking my understanding, not asserting it. That matters because it keeps the speaker in control of their own meaning.

Critical: always leave room for correction End your reflection with a slight upward tone or an implicit question — not a statement. "It sounds like the timeline is what's worrying you?" is better than "So you're worried about the timeline." The first invites correction. The second closes it.

Two types of reflection

There are two things you can reflect: what someone said (content), or how they seemed to feel about it (emotion). Both are useful. Emotion reflections are higher-risk, higher-reward.

Content reflection

Paraphrase the facts and information. Lower risk. Good default in professional settings.

"So the issue is that the data isn't ready in time for the Thursday deadline?"
Emotion reflection

Name the feeling underneath what was said. More powerful — but only do this if you're fairly sure you've read it right.

"It sounds like you've been sitting with this problem for a while and are pretty frustrated with the lack of movement?"

In most professional meetings, start with content reflection. Move to emotion reflection when you sense the stakes are high for the other person — frustration, stress, or something that clearly matters to them.

A real example

Here's what this looks like in a meeting. A colleague says:

👤
Colleague
"I keep raising this dependency with the other team and they just say they'll get to it. We've been blocked for two weeks. I don't know what else I can do at this point."

Most responses skip the reflection entirely and jump to advice: "Have you tried escalating to their manager?" or "We could just work around it."

A content reflection first: "So you've been blocked for two weeks and you've already tried flagging it — and it's still not moving?"

An emotion reflection: "Sounds like you're feeling a bit stuck — like you've done the right thing and it's still not working?"

Either of these does something the advice didn't: it confirms you understood the actual situation before you try to fix it. And more often than not, the speaker will respond by adding something new — "Yeah, and the thing is, it's going to affect the whole Q3 plan if it goes on much longer." That's the real problem. You wouldn't have heard it if you'd jumped straight to the workaround.

What reflective listening is not

🦜
Parroting
Repeating their exact words back sounds robotic and creates no value. It signals you heard the sounds, not the meaning.
❌ Them: "I'm worried about the deadline." You: "You're worried about the deadline."
✓ "It sounds like the timeline is the main pressure right now?"
✅
Agreement
Reflecting someone's view does not mean you agree with it. You can accurately capture what someone said and then disagree — but only after you've proven you understood it first.
❌ "So what you're saying is X… but I disagree, because—"
✓ "So what you're saying is X? [pause for confirmation] Here's my concern with that approach…"
💊
A ticket to giving advice
Reflecting once and then launching into a long solution misses the point. The reflection creates space — honour it. Ask a question, or wait to see if they continue. The advice can come after.
❌ "It sounds like you're blocked. Anyway, here's what I'd do: [three paragraphs]"
✓ "It sounds like you're blocked. What's your read on why the other team keeps deprioritising it?"

Practice: pick the best reflection

Four meeting scenarios. For each, pick the option that best reflects what the speaker said. You'll get immediate feedback on why.

Which response is the best reflection?

The skill to carry into your next meeting

Once per meeting, before you give your response to anything, say one reflective sentence first. Not every time — just once. Pick the moment that feels most important. Notice what the other person does next.

What you've learned

  • Reflective listening = paraphrase first, respond second
  • Content reflection captures facts; emotion reflection captures feeling — use both, but start with content
  • It is not parroting, not agreeing, and not a preamble to a lecture
  • The reflection creates space — and that space usually surfaces what the person actually meant
