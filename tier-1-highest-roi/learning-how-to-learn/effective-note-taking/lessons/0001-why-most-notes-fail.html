Why Most Notes Fail

The single mistake that makes most notes useless — and the one shift that fixes it.

What you'll learn

  • Why copying words into notes is almost always wasted effort
  • The "transcription trap" and why it feels productive but isn't
  • The one principle that makes notes actually useful
  • What a good note looks like vs. a bad one — with real examples

The problem with most notes

Most people take notes like this: something interesting appears — a sentence in a book, a slide in a presentation, a key point in a video — and they copy it down. Word for word, or close to it.

The problem isn't the copying. It's what copying doesn't do: it doesn't require you to understand anything. You can transcribe a sentence without processing it at all. And if you didn't process it, the note can't help you later — because you don't actually know what it means.

This is the transcription trap: the illusion that capturing something is the same as learning it. It feels productive. You have pages of notes. But when you come back a week later, you can't remember the context, you don't understand your own writing, and you never look at it again. (Tiago Forte, Building a Second Brain)

The one principle that changes everything

Here it is:

The core principle A note is not a copy of someone else's words. A note is your thinking about someone else's idea.

This means the value isn't in capturing the source's words — it's in recording what you understood, what it connects to, what it means for you, and what questions it raises. That's what makes a note retrievable and useful. (Cornell University Learning Strategies Center)

German sociologist Niklas Luhmann wrote over 90,000 cards of notes over his lifetime and produced more than 70 books and 400 articles. His method? Never copy. Always write in your own words. Always link to another idea. The note had to represent his understanding, not the source's words. (Zettelkasten.de)

What this looks like in practice

Here's the same piece of information, captured two different ways:

Transcription (bad)

"The hippocampus is central to the consolidation of information from short-term memory to long-term memory, and is also important for spatial navigation."

Your thinking (good)

The hippocampus is basically the brain's filing clerk — it decides what goes to long-term storage. Damage it and you can't form new memories (like H.M. case). Also handles maps in the head — makes sense why spatial memory feels linked to episodic memory.

The second note is messier, less precise — and far more useful. It shows you processed it. When you come back a week later, that note triggers a real memory. The first one is just a quotation you don't own.

A simple structure to start with: The Cornell Method

You don't need a complex system. The Cornell Method is one of the most well-studied note-taking formats in existence, and it has one job: force you to process what you've captured. (Cornell Learning Strategies Center)

Cornell Note Structure
Cue Column
Questions and keywords — written after you take the main notes. "What was the main point?" "What problem does this solve?" These are your retrieval triggers.
Notes Column
Your understanding in your own words — not copies. Abbreviate, use your own examples, draw connections. If you wrote this from memory, would it still make sense?
Summary
2–3 sentences at the bottom — written after the session. "The main idea here is..." This is the hardest part and the most valuable.

You don't need to use Cornell format strictly. The key insight is its sequence: capture first (in your own words), then add cues, then summarise. Each step forces a bit more processing.

Your one action from this lesson Next time you take a note from anything — a book, a video, a conversation — write one sentence about it in your own words before you write anything else. Not a copy. Your interpretation. That single habit is the foundation.

2. Which of these is the better note on the idea that "habits form when a behaviour is consistently rewarded"?

A "Habits form when a behaviour is consistently rewarded." (copied verbatim)
B "Brain links action → reward. Repeat = habit. This explains why checking phone is so sticky — the variable reward (like slot machine) is especially powerful."
C "Habits. Rewards. Behaviour. Consistent."
D A perfectly formatted bullet point with source citation and page number
Yes. Option B shows thinking — a connection to an existing idea (slot machine variable rewards), a personal inference, and shortened language that proves you own the idea. That note is retrievable and useful.
The best note here is B. It's messy but shows real processing — a personal connection, an inference, shortened language. That's what makes it retrievable. A copied sentence or fragmented keywords prove nothing was understood.

3. In the Cornell Method, what is the purpose of the "Cue Column"?

A To write down the source title and page numbers for citation
B To colour-code your notes by topic for visual organisation
C To write questions and keywords that act as retrieval triggers — added after the notes are taken
D To summarise the whole session in 2–3 sentences
Correct. Cues are added after — not during — note-taking. They're questions or keywords designed to help you retrieve the content later without re-reading everything.
The Cue Column contains retrieval triggers: questions like "What was the main point?" or keywords that prompt you to recall the note without re-reading it. The summary box (at the bottom) is for the 2–3 sentence overview.
