The Listening Gap

Why your brain is wired to miss what people actually mean — and which pattern is costing you the most in meetings.

There is a gap in every conversation

When someone speaks in a meeting, they deliver about 125 words per minute. Your brain can process language at 400–500 words per minute. That leaves a surplus of roughly 275–375 words per minute of unused mental capacity.

That surplus doesn't stay empty. Your brain fills it with something — and whatever it fills it with is the reason you miss what people actually mean.

Three ways the gap gets filled

Most people fall into one dominant pattern. Recognising yours is the first step to changing it.

✂️
The Interrupter
Locks onto a point and can't wait. Jumps in before the speaker finishes — often finishing their sentences or steering to a new thread. The spare capacity fills with urgency to speak.
Tell-tale sign: You often start your response with "Yes, and—" or "Right, but—" within a breath of the other person stopping.
🌫️
The Zoner
Physically present, mentally elsewhere. Picks up the thread intermittently but misses the connective tissue — the nuance between points. The spare capacity fills with unrelated thought.
Tell-tale sign: You've asked someone to repeat something they said 30 seconds ago, or you're tracking "when is this meeting ending?"
🔧
The Solution Jumper
Hears the problem and immediately shifts into fix-it mode. Stops listening to understand and starts listening to confirm a solution already forming. The spare capacity fills with your answer.
Tell-tale sign: You can articulate your response before the speaker has finished explaining the problem.
The cost in meetings When you're filling the gap with your own content, you're hearing the speaker's words — but you're missing their meaning. The real message (what they actually need, fear, or are trying to signal) lives in the parts you're not attending to.

Three levels of listening

The Co-Active Training Institute — one of the most widely-used coaching frameworks in the world — maps listening onto three levels. Most people in meetings operate at Level 1, almost always.

1
Internal Listening
Your attention is on yourself — your reactions, your judgements, what you want to say next. You hear the speaker, but you're filtering everything through your own frame.
"They're saying X, which reminds me of Y, and I think I should say Z."
2
Focused Listening
Your attention is fully on the other person. You're noticing their words, their tone, what they choose to say and not say. This is where understanding begins.
"They said 'a bit frustrated' — but the way they said it sounded more like exhausted."
3
Global Listening
Your attention expands to the whole environment — energy in the room, what's not being said, the underlying tension. Advanced skill, used selectively.
"The whole team went quiet when she said that. Something landed differently than intended."

Framework: Three Levels of Listening — Co-Active Training Institute

The goal for now You don't need to live at Level 3. The mission is to catch yourself at Level 1 and shift to Level 2, especially in the moments that matter in a meeting.

Self-assessment: What fills your gap?

Answer these 5 quick scenarios honestly. You'll get a personalised micro-practice to take into your next meeting.

Which listener are you?
5 scenarios. Pick the option that sounds most like you — even if none is perfect.
Question 1 of 5
A colleague is describing a problem they ran into. Midway through, you already have a solution in mind. What happens next?
Question 2 of 5
Someone is giving a long update in a meeting. At what point do you notice your attention drifting?
Question 3 of 5
After a meeting, a colleague asks "what do you think Sarah was really worried about?" How confident are you?
Question 4 of 5
Someone raises a concern and you immediately think "we've tried that before, it doesn't work." What do you do?
Question 5 of 5
Which of these would colleagues most likely say about you in meetings?
✂️

Your pattern: The Interrupter

You're highly engaged — which is actually the root of the problem. Your brain moves fast, you form strong views, and silence feels like wasted opportunity. The gap fills with urgency.

🌫️

Your pattern: The Zoner

You're not disengaged by nature — you just haven't been given enough reason to stay present. The gap fills with whatever else is on your mind, which is usually a lot.

🔧

Your pattern: The Solution Jumper

You're a natural problem-solver — which is an asset everywhere except the moment you need to actually understand the problem. The gap fills with your answer before the question is complete.

Your micro-practice for the next meeting

When you feel the urge to speak — pause for exactly two seconds after the other person stops. Count silently: one, two. Only then open your mouth. That pause will feel uncomfortable at first. That discomfort is the practice. You'll find the speaker often continues and says something more important.

Your micro-practice for the next meeting

Before your next meeting, pick one person and commit to tracking: what does this person actually need from this meeting? Write it down after. Not what was discussed — what they needed. That question gives your brain something to do with the spare capacity, and it will automatically pull you back when you drift.

Your micro-practice for the next meeting

When someone describes a problem, your job for the first 60 seconds is to listen for the feeling behind the problem — not the facts of it. Are they frustrated? Uncertain? Overwhelmed? Find that before you reach for a solution. A single question like "how long has this been a problem?" will surface more than three minutes of advice.

What you've learned

  • The listening gap is structural — it's not a character flaw
  • Everyone fills the gap with something; your pattern is your default
  • Level 1 listening (focused on yourself) is the default for most people in meetings
  • Shifting to Level 2 (focused on the speaker) is the goal — not eliminating your thoughts, but redirecting attention
