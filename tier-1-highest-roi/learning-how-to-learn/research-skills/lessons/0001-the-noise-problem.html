The Noise Problem

Why the internet makes bad information easy to find — and the four-step method for cutting through it fast.

What you'll learn

Why bad information is easy to find

It's tempting to think misinformation is a fringe problem — that a quick Google search mostly returns reliable results. But the internet's economics work against you: engagement drives visibility, and outrage, fear, and novelty drive engagement more than accuracy does. (Web Literacy for Student Fact-Checkers, Mike Caulfield)

The result: the same question can return wildly different answers depending on what you search, who wrote the first result, and when it was published. "Finding information" is easy. Finding reliable information requires a different skill — source evaluation.

How unreliable content disguises itself

Bad sources rarely look obviously bad. They use confident language, professional formatting, and real-sounding statistics. The most common deception patterns:

  • Outdated information presented as current — a 2014 study cited as if it settles a 2024 question
  • Misleading statistics — technically true numbers that leave out crucial context
  • Authority borrowing — a low-credibility site citing a high-credibility institution to seem legitimate
  • Vague sourcing — "studies show" or "experts say" with no links or names
  • Emotional framing — language designed to trigger reaction, not inform

The first step isn't learning to detect each of these patterns — it's learning to pause before trusting anything. This is the foundation of the SIFT method. (Caulfield, Web Literacy)

The SIFT method

SIFT was developed by digital literacy educator Mike Caulfield and is now used by fact-checkers and universities worldwide. It gives you a fast, reliable process for evaluating any source in under two minutes.

S
Stop
Pause before you react, share, or cite. Ask: "Do I know if this source is reliable?" If not — stop and check. This alone prevents most errors.
I
Investigate the source
Before reading the article in depth — open a new tab and search the source itself. What is this publication? Who runs it? What's their track record?
F
Find better coverage
Look for the same claim covered by other sources. If something is true and important, multiple independent outlets will have covered it. Lone claims are a red flag.
T
Trace claims to origin
Follow the source chain. If an article says "a study found X," find the actual study. Often the original says something more nuanced — or different entirely.
This lesson focuses on S and I only Stop and Investigate are the two highest-leverage steps — most bad sources fail at the "I" stage. Master these first. Lessons 2 and 3 cover Find and Trace.

How to Investigate a source in 60 seconds

When you land on an unfamiliar site, don't read the article first. Open a new tab and search: [site name] + "bias" or "reliability" or "Wikipedia".

What to look for:

  • Wikipedia entry — most major publications have one. Check if it mentions bias, funding, or controversy.
  • Media Bias/Fact Check — mediabiasfactcheck.com rates thousands of news sources for reliability and political lean.
  • About page — does it clearly say who runs this, who funds it, and what their purpose is? Vague "About" pages are a red flag.
  • Author credentials — is the author named? Can you find them elsewhere? Do they have relevant expertise?

Self-Check

Test what you just learned

1. Why does misinformation spread faster than accurate information online?

A Because people are generally gullible and don't think critically
B Because engagement drives visibility — and emotion-triggering content gets more shares than accurate but neutral content
C Because search engines are deliberately biased toward false information
D Because false information is always more interesting than true information
Exactly. The incentive structure of social media rewards engagement, not accuracy. Outrage and novelty spread faster than nuance. This isn't a moral failing — it's the platform's design.
The core issue is structural: engagement algorithms amplify content that triggers reactions (outrage, fear, surprise) regardless of accuracy. It's not about gullibility — it's about how the economics of attention work online.

2. In SIFT, what does the "I" (Investigate) step tell you to do?

A Read the article carefully and internally check whether it seems logical
B Look at the comments section to see what others think
C Open a new tab and look up the source itself — who runs it, their reputation, and whether they're known to be reliable
D Investigate the individual claim by searching for counter-arguments
Yes. And critically — you do this before reading the article in depth. Once you've read something persuasive, it's cognitively harder to dismiss it even if the source turns out to be unreliable. Investigate first.
The Investigate step means researching the source itself — in a new tab, before you read the article. You're asking: who is this? Are they known to be reliable? This tells you how much trust to extend before you've even read the content.

3. An article claims "Scientists find coffee extends lifespan by 10 years." It's on a site called coffeehealthbenefits.com with no author listed. What's the most important first move?

A Read the article thoroughly to check whether the argument is convincing
B Share it with a note saying "needs verification"
C Stop — then investigate "coffeehealthbenefits.com" in a new tab before reading further
D Search for the specific study mentioned to see if it exists
Correct. The site name alone is a red flag (named after the conclusion it promotes). Before searching for the study, investigate the source. A site dedicated to promoting coffee health benefits has a clear incentive to publish favourable (and potentially distorted) findings.
The first step is always Stop, then Investigate the source. A domain called "coffeehealthbenefits.com" has a named agenda before you've read a word. Investigate the site itself first — don't let the article's argument reach you before you know whether to trust it.
