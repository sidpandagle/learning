The Forgetting Curve

Why your brain erases almost everything you learn — and the simple fix that changes everything.

What you'll learn

  • Why forgetting is not a flaw — it's a feature of how memory works
  • What the forgetting curve is and what it predicts
  • The two forces that fight the curve: review and retrieval
  • How this knowledge is the foundation of your learning system

The uncomfortable truth about studying

You read a chapter. You feel like you understand it. You close the book. Three days later, you remember almost none of it.

This isn't because you're bad at learning. It's because of how human memory actually works — and once you understand it, you can design around it.

In 1885, a German psychologist named Hermann Ebbinghaus sat down and memorised hundreds of meaningless syllable sequences. Then he tested himself repeatedly over weeks, tracking exactly how much he forgot over time. What he found became one of the most replicated findings in all of cognitive science: the forgetting curve. (Recallify, 2024)

What the curve tells us

The forgetting curve is brutal. Without any deliberate effort to remember:

20 minutes
You've already forgotten ~42% of what you just learned
The steepest drop happens right away
1 hour
About 56% is gone
The curve flattens slightly but keeps falling
1 day
You retain only ~33%
Most of it is gone by the next morning
1 week
Down to ~20%
After a week without review, the material is nearly gone
Key insight This is why "I read the whole book" rarely translates into lasting knowledge. Reading once is not learning — it's just first exposure.

Forgetting is not your enemy

Here's what most people miss: forgetting isn't a bug in your brain — it's a feature. Your brain has limited resources, and it constantly prunes information it thinks you don't need. If you never use a piece of knowledge, your brain reasonably concludes: "This isn't important." And it lets it go.

The implication? You need to signal to your brain that something is worth keeping. The way you do that is by revisiting and retrieving it — repeatedly, over time.

Ebbinghaus himself discovered this. When he reviewed the same syllables multiple times, the forgetting curve flattened dramatically. Each review made the memory stronger and the forgetting slower. (Spaced Repetition in STEM, ERIC)

Two forces that fight the curve

Modern learning science has identified two techniques that directly combat the forgetting curve. We'll dedicate full lessons to each — but here's the overview:

2×
Spaced Repetition
Review at increasing intervals (today → 3 days → 1 week → 2 weeks)
50%
Active Recall
Testing yourself remembers 50% more after a week vs. rereading

Spaced repetition is the scheduling strategy: you review material right before you're about to forget it, which resets the curve each time. (Recallify)

Active recall is the method of review: instead of rereading, you test yourself — forcing your brain to retrieve the information. A landmark 2006 study by Roediger and Karpicke showed that students who tested themselves once remembered 50% more after a week than students who reread the material three times. (Make It Stick, via Shortform)

The core principle of your learning system Every technique you'll learn in this workspace is built on one idea: learning happens during retrieval, not during exposure. The act of trying to remember something is what makes memories stick. We'll return to this idea in every lesson.

What this means for you right now

You don't need to overhaul everything at once. The one immediate takeaway from this lesson:

Schedule a review. Whatever you learn today, plan to test yourself on it in 24 hours. Not re-read — test. Close the material and ask yourself: "What do I remember? What was the main point? What was surprising?"

That single habit, done consistently, puts you ahead of 90% of learners who trust passive exposure to do the work.
