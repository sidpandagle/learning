Your AI Workflow

Building AI into your daily work so it's a reflex, not an afterthought.

Why most people stay "occasional AI users"

Most people who try AI and find it useful don't actually build it into their workflow. They use it occasionally — when they remember, or when a task feels like an obvious AI job. The result is inconsistent value: sometimes transformative, often forgotten.

The gap between occasional users and people who get consistent value from AI is rarely skill. It's system. Occasional users rely on memory and impulse. Systematic users have three things: triggers that prompt them to reach for AI, templates that capture their best prompts, and review habits that keep their outputs reliable.

The three-layer system Triggers → Templates → Review. Each layer builds on the last. Triggers get you to reach for AI consistently. Templates get you to a useful output fast. Review habits keep quality high.

The workflow loop

The AI Workflow Loop
Five steps that repeat for every task where you use AI.
1
Trigger — recognise this as an AI task
A recurring task hits your desk. Your trigger list fires and you reach for AI rather than starting from scratch manually.
2
Prompt — use a template or build one
Pull a saved template and adapt it with the task-specific context. If no template exists, build the prompt from scratch using RCTF.
3
Output — get the first draft
AI produces the output. You receive it knowing it's a 70–80% draft, not a finished product.
4
Review — verify, edit, own it
Check facts, reread in the reader's voice, edit to sound like you. The output leaves as yours, not the AI's.
5
Refine template — save what worked
If the prompt produced a strong output, save or update the template. Each good session compounds your future speed.

Layer 1: Triggers

A trigger is a condition that reliably tells you "this task is worth handing to AI." Without triggers, you rely on remembering — and memory fails under load, which is exactly when you most need AI's help.

The best triggers are task-type triggers: recurring categories of work that consistently benefit from AI generation. They look like this:

  • Every time I need to write a first draft of anything longer than a paragraph
  • Every time I need to summarise something longer than 2 pages
  • Every time I need to brainstorm more than 5 options
  • Every time I need to reformat content from one structure to another
  • Every time I need to explain something technical to a non-technical audience

You don't need many triggers — three to five consistent ones are more valuable than a long list you won't remember.

Layer 2: Templates

A prompt template is a saved prompt with placeholders for the parts that change. You build templates by capturing the prompts that worked well — the ones that produced a useful output in one or two tries.

Why templates are 10x leverage The time investment in a great prompt pays back every time you reuse it. A prompt that took 5 minutes to craft and saves 20 minutes per use breaks even after 15 minutes of total reuse. After ten uses, it's paid back an hour. Save your best prompts.

A template doesn't need to be elaborate. It just needs to have the RCTF structure filled in for the recurring role and context, with [TASK-SPECIFIC DETAILS] as a placeholder for what changes each time. For example:

Example template: Weekly status update You are a clear, direct communicator writing to a busy manager who skims emails.

Context: I'm an [ROLE] at [COMPANY]. My manager cares most about [WHAT THEY CARE ABOUT].

Write a weekly status update covering: what I completed this week, what's blocked, and what I'm doing next week. Lead with the most important item. Keep it under 150 words. Use plain prose — no bullet points.

Layer 3: Review habits

Every AI output you send out carries your name. The review layer is what separates AI-assisted work that builds trust from AI-assisted work that damages it.

Three review checks before any AI output leaves your hands:

Check 1
Fact-check the specifics
Any number, date, name, citation, or statistic — verify it. These are where hallucinations concentrate. The rest of the output may be fine; the specific claims are the risk.
Check 2
Reread in the reader's voice
Before sending, read the output as the recipient would. Does the tone land correctly for them? Does it answer their actual question? Does it sound like something a real person would write?
Check 3
Edit to own it
Make at least one edit — a word change, a sentence restructured, a point reordered. This keeps your judgment active, personalises the output, and ensures you actually read it rather than skim.

The weekly AI audit

Once a week, spend 5 minutes on three questions:

  1. What tasks did AI help with this week? Reinforce the triggers that worked.
  2. What output missed and why? Identify a prompt to improve or a context to add.
  3. What prompt is worth saving as a template? Capture it before you forget the exact wording.

Five minutes. Done weekly. This compounds over months into a personal AI system tailored to exactly how you work.

Course recap: what you've learned

1
The AI Mental Model
AI is a prediction engine. The context window is its entire world. Use the capable intern model to set the right expectations and briefing instincts.
2
Prompt Architecture
RCTF: Role, Context, Task, Format. Put context before the request. When an output misses, identify which element was missing — that's almost always the fix.
3
When to Use AI
AI wins on generation tasks; struggles on judgment tasks. The 2×2 triage keeps you in the right quadrant: delegate freely, use with review, assist your judgment, or skip.
4
Iterating Effectively
Diagnose first, correct surgically, change one thing at a time. The 80% threshold: if output is more than 20% off, restart with a better prompt rather than iterate.
5
Your AI Workflow
Triggers → Templates → Review. The weekly audit compounds your system. Consistent value comes from habit, not from individual sessions.
