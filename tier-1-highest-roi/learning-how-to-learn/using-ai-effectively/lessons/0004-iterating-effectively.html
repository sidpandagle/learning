Iterating Effectively

How to get from a mediocre first output to a great final result in two steps, not ten.

Why iteration spirals

The most common failure mode in AI work isn't a bad first output. It's what happens after: the user types "make it better," or "that's not quite right," or "try again." The AI produces something slightly different. The user tries again. Ten rounds later they're further from what they wanted than when they started.

This spiral happens because vague feedback produces vague improvement. The model has no way to know what specifically is wrong — so it varies the output in whatever direction feels most probable. Without a clear diagnosis, each iteration is a random walk.

The spiral pattern Vague feedback → vague change → different problem → more vague feedback → more vague change → frustration → giving up or accepting mediocre output.

The two-step iteration model

Good iteration has two distinct steps — and most people skip the first one.

Step 1: Diagnose what's wrong, specifically. Before typing a follow-up, identify the actual problem. Is the tone wrong? Is it too long? Does it miss the point? Are specific facts incorrect? Is the structure right but the depth wrong? Name it precisely before you type anything.

Step 2: Give surgical correction. Feedback that maps directly onto the diagnosis. Not "make it more professional" — instead "replace the conversational tone with formal business English, keep the structure the same." Not "it's too long" — instead "cut the second paragraph entirely and reduce the third to two sentences."

The one-thing-at-a-time rule Change one thing per iteration. If you change tone, format, and content simultaneously, you can't tell what worked. When the next output is better, you don't know why — so you can't repeat it. When it's worse, you don't know what caused it. One variable at a time turns iterations into learnable experiments.

The diagnostic questions

When an output misses, run through this list before responding:

Tone: Is the voice wrong — too formal, too casual, too generic, wrong register for the audience?
Scope: Is it too broad (touched everything shallowly) or too narrow (missed the key points I needed)?
Format: Is the structure wrong — bullets when I needed prose, paragraphs when I needed a list?
Depth: Is it too surface-level, or did it over-explain things I already know?
Facts: Are specific claims wrong, missing, or unverified?
Missing element: Did it answer the wrong question, or answer the right question but omit something critical?

When to abandon and restart

Not every output deserves another iteration. Sometimes the first output is so far off that iterating is slower than starting fresh. The 80% threshold: if the output is less than 80% of the way there, consider restarting with a better initial prompt rather than trying to patch it.

Signs it's time to restart:

  • You've iterated more than three times and it keeps missing in different ways
  • The fundamental structure or approach is wrong (hard to fix with a follow-up)
  • The initial prompt was missing important context that would've changed the whole output
The context reset technique When starting over, don't just rephrase the same prompt. Start a new conversation entirely. Long conversation threads can confuse the model — earlier turns accumulate context that sometimes works against you. A fresh conversation with a better prompt often outperforms ten rounds of iteration.

Your failure mode: Vague feedback

You're giving the model something to react to but not enough to act on. The fix is to name the specific problem before you type the follow-up.

Your failure mode: Scope creep

You're adding requirements mid-stream that change the nature of the task. The fix is to clarify what you need before starting, not during iteration.

You're occasionally reaching for AI on tasks outside its reliable range. The fix is the Lesson 3 triage — match the task to the quadrant before starting.

Your failure mode: Over-iterating

You keep refining past the point of diminishing returns instead of restarting. The fix is the 80% threshold — when an output is more than 20% off, a fresh prompt beats ten more rounds.

Your iteration practice

Before your next follow-up, write the specific problem as a single sentence using one of the six diagnostic dimensions: tone, scope, format, depth, facts, or missing element. Then write your correction in the same sentence. "The tone is too casual for a legal audience — rewrite in formal business English without changing the structure."

Your iteration practice

Before starting a significant AI task, spend 2 minutes writing out what "done" looks like — the audience, the format, the must-have elements. This front-loads the clarity work and prevents scope from shifting mid-stream. If you discover a new requirement during iteration, note it separately and address it in a clean final pass.

Your iteration practice

When an iteration chain isn't working, pause and ask: "Is this a generation task or a judgment task?" If it's judgment, no amount of iteration will fix it — you need to step in and own the content. Use AI for structure and first pass, then rewrite from your own expertise.

Your iteration practice

Set a personal rule: if you've iterated three times and the output still misses by more than 20%, stop. Open a new conversation. Rewrite the prompt from scratch using what you've learned from the failed attempts. The third-attempt prompt, written with that knowledge, almost always outperforms round four of an existing chain.

What you've learned

  • Vague feedback produces vague improvement — iteration spirals start here
  • Good iteration: diagnose specifically first, then give surgical correction second
  • Change one thing at a time so you can tell what worked
  • The 80% threshold: when output is more than 20% off, restart rather than patch
  • Long conversation threads can work against you — a fresh context often beats round ten
