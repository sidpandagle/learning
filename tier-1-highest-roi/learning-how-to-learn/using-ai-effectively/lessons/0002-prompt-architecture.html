Prompt Architecture

The four elements every strong prompt has — and how to build them in the right order.

Why most prompts fail before you finish typing them

The most common prompting mistake isn't asking the wrong thing. It's asking the right thing in the wrong order — leading with the request and leaving out everything the model needs to do it well.

Think about how you'd brief the capable intern from Lesson 1. You wouldn't walk up and say "write me an email." You'd say: "You're helping our sales team. We're pitching to mid-market companies. Write a follow-up email to a prospect who went quiet after a demo — keep it short and don't be pushy."

That's not over-explaining. That's a functional briefing. And it's exactly what a strong prompt looks like.

The RCTF framework Every effective prompt has four components: Role, Context, Task, and Format. You don't need all four every time — but knowing which one is missing is how you diagnose a failing prompt.

The four elements

R
Role — who should the AI be?
Setting a role primes the model to respond from a particular perspective, vocabulary, and expertise level. It filters out noise and focuses the response on what that role would actually say.
"You are a senior copywriter who specialises in B2B SaaS." / "Act as a sceptical investor reviewing this pitch." / "You're an expert explainer writing for non-technical readers."
C
Context — what does the AI need to know?
The background facts that make the difference between a generic and a useful output. Who is the audience? What's the situation? What has already been tried? What constraints exist? This is the briefing.
"Our audience is operations managers at logistics companies. They're sceptical of new software and have been burned by failed implementations before."
T
Task — what exactly do you want?
The specific request — an action verb, a scope, and any constraints. Vague tasks get vague outputs. The task should be specific enough that you'd know the difference between a good result and a bad one.
"Write a 150-word LinkedIn post announcing our product launch. Avoid superlatives. End with a clear call to action."
F
Format — how should the output look?
Without a format instruction, the model defaults to whatever structure is most common for that type of request. Explicit format instructions are the fastest way to get exactly what you need, first try.
"Return as a numbered list of 5 items, each with a one-sentence explanation." / "Write in plain paragraphs, no bullet points." / "Use a table with columns: Task, Owner, Due Date."

Seeing it in action: before and after

Here's the same request rebuilt with RCTF:

Without RCTF
Write a summary of this project for stakeholders.
With RCTF
[Role] You are a project manager writing for senior executives who don't read below the fold.

[Context] We've just completed a 3-month migration to a new data pipeline. It came in on time but 15% over budget. Two major issues were discovered and resolved mid-project.

[Task] Write a project close-out summary that leads with outcomes, acknowledges the budget overage honestly, and explains the key decisions made.

[Format] Three short paragraphs. No bullet points. Under 200 words total.
You don't always need all four For simple tasks — "fix the grammar in this paragraph" or "translate this to Spanish" — RCTF is overkill. The framework is a diagnostic tool. When an output is missing the mark, ask: which element was I missing? That's almost always the fix.
Order matters Lead with Role and Context before the Task. The model processes your prompt sequentially — if you start with the request, it begins generating before it has the briefing it needs. Put the context first, task second, format last.

🎭

Your gap: Missing Role

You tend to jump straight to task and context but skip setting who the AI should be. Role shapes the vocabulary, perspective, and depth of the response.

📋

Your gap: Missing Context

You write clear requests but leave out the briefing — who's involved, what's already been tried, what the constraints are. That's the information that turns generic into useful.

🎯

Your gap: Vague Task

You give good background but leave the actual ask too open. "Tell me what's going on" and "give me ideas" are starting points, not tasks. Sharpen the verb and add scope constraints.

📐

Your gap: Missing Format

You know what you want but don't specify how you want it delivered. Without format instructions, you get the average output shape — which is often not what you need.

Your micro-practice for the next prompt

Before you write your next prompt, spend 5 seconds deciding: who should be answering this? Add one sentence starting with "You are a..." that captures the relevant expertise and perspective. You'll immediately notice the output changes in depth and vocabulary.

Your micro-practice for the next prompt

Before submitting your next prompt, ask yourself: "What would a smart new colleague need to know to handle this well?" Write that as two or three sentences before the task. Audience, situation, constraints — put them all in. The model will use all of it.

Your micro-practice for the next prompt

Rewrite your task as: [action verb] + [specific scope] + [one key constraint]. For example: "Write a 200-word email that declines the meeting without closing the door." The combination of verb + scope + constraint makes the task answerable in one shot.

Your micro-practice for the next prompt

End every prompt with a format instruction, even a simple one. "Return as a bulleted list." "Keep it under 100 words." "Use a table." It takes 3 seconds and saves you an iteration round almost every time.

What you've learned

  • Most prompts fail because they lead with the request and skip the briefing
  • RCTF: Role, Context, Task, Format — the four elements of a strong prompt
  • Order matters: context before task, task before format
  • You don't need all four every time — but when an output misses, check which element is missing
