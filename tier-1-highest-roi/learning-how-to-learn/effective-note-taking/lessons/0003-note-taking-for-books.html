Note-Taking for Books

Books give you the rarest gift in information: time. Here's how to use it to take notes that last.

What you'll learn

  • Why the "note while reading" approach usually backfires
  • The Read → Pause → Note cycle that actually works
  • A simple filter: what's worth noting from a book, and what to skip
  • The one-page book summary — a template you can use for every book you finish

Books are different

Unlike meetings or videos, books move at your pace. You can stop, re-read, and think. This is an advantage — but most people squander it by taking notes the same way they do with fast-paced content: constantly, reactively, with no filter.

The result: pages of notes that mirror the book's structure instead of your understanding of it. You've done transcription work, not thinking work.

Books require a deliberate read-pause-note cycle, not continuous annotation. (Farnam Street — How to Read a Book)

The Read → Pause → Note cycle

The single most important shift you can make when reading books:

Read
A section or chapter. No notes yet.
→
Pause
Close the book. What do you remember?
→
Note
Write what stuck — in your words.

The pause is everything. It forces retrieval — you have to actively pull out what you understood rather than passively copying what you just read. What survives the pause is what mattered. What you can't recall wasn't processed deeply enough to be worth noting.

Why not note while reading? Because highlighting and noting while reading feel like learning but produce no retrieval. Your brain is in receiving mode. The Pause step switches it to retrieval mode — which is where memory consolidation actually happens. (The Learning Scientists)

What's worth noting — and what to skip

Not every idea in a book deserves a note. Most books have one or two core arguments, supported by examples, data, and stories. The examples are illustrations — once you've understood the idea, you don't need them.

Note it

  • Ideas that surprised you
  • Arguments that changed your view
  • Concepts you can apply directly
  • Connections to things you already know
  • Questions the book raised but didn't answer

Skip it

  • Things you already knew
  • Examples that just illustrate a point you got
  • Tangential stories and anecdotes
  • Repeated points from earlier chapters
  • Background context you don't need

A good rule: if a note doesn't change anything you'd think or do, skip it. You're looking for the ideas that update your model of how something works.

What about marginalia?

Underlining and marginalia (notes in the margins) are useful for marking passages in the moment — but they're not a substitute for your own synthesis. (Farnam Street)

Use margins for quick symbols during reading:

  • ★ — this matters, return to it
  • ? — I don't understand this or I disagree
  • ! — this surprised me
  • → — this connects to something else I know

Then, in the Pause step, convert your marked passages into proper notes. The margin marks are a capture tool — the notes are the processed output.

The one-page book summary

When you finish a book, create one artifact: a single-page summary. This is the note you'll actually return to. Every other note from the book feeds into it.

One-Page Book Summary Template
Title & Author
The book's full title and the author's name.
The core argument
One to two sentences: what is the book's main claim? What is it trying to convince you of?
3–5 key ideas
The most important concepts — explained in your own words, not the author's. Each in 1–3 sentences.
What I'm changing
One specific thing you'll do differently because of this book. If there's nothing — that's worth noting too.
Best quote
One line from the book that captures something important — the one you'd share with someone else.
Rating / revisit?
A personal rating (1–5) and whether you'd re-read it. Useful for recommending later.

This template takes 10–15 minutes to fill in after finishing a book. The thinking it forces — especially "what am I changing?" — is more valuable than all the chapter-by-chapter notes combined.

Your one action from this lesson For your next book or long article, try the Read → Pause → Note cycle on a single chapter. Close the book after the chapter, recall what you remember, then write it in your own words. Then use your margin marks to fill gaps. Notice how different it feels from noting while reading.

Test what you just learned

1. Why is the Pause step in the Read → Pause → Note cycle so important?

A It gives you time to look up words you didn't understand while reading
B It forces retrieval — you must actively recall what you understood rather than passively copying what you just read
C It lets your eyes rest so you can read more in a single session
D It helps you decide which chapters to skip in the rest of the book
Exactly. The pause switches your brain from receiving mode to retrieval mode. What survives the pause is what your brain actually processed. What you can't recall is what slipped through without being understood.
The pause forces retrieval — it requires you to pull out what you understood, rather than copying what you just read. This is the moment where passive reading becomes active learning. Memory consolidation happens during retrieval, not during reading.

2. You're reading a chapter about pricing psychology. The author tells a long story about a wine merchant to illustrate that presentation affects perceived value. You already understood that principle before the story. What should you do?

A Note the story anyway — specific examples help you remember the principle
B Skip the story in your notes — you already have the concept, and the example didn't update anything
C Highlight the story and return to it later when reviewing
D Note the story title in case you want to look it up
Correct. If you already knew the principle and the story didn't update or deepen your understanding, it's not worth a note. Notes are for ideas that change something — not for confirming what you already believed.
If you already understood the principle and the story added nothing new, skip it. Notes are for ideas that update your understanding — not for collecting examples of things you already knew. The filter question is: did this change anything I think or do?

3. What is the most important field in the one-page book summary template — and why?

A The best quote — because a memorable line is the easiest thing to share with others
B The core argument — because summarising the book's thesis proves you understood it
C What I'm changing — because if the book doesn't change anything you think or do, it didn't really teach you anything
D The 3–5 key ideas — because these contain the most substantive information from the book
Yes. "What I'm changing" is the ultimate test of learning. Understanding a concept intellectually is different from it actually changing your behaviour or thinking. If you can't name something specific, the book is knowledge without application.
The most powerful field is "What I'm changing." Understanding is not the same as learning — learning changes something. If you can't name a single thing you'll do or think differently, the book hasn't moved you from knowledge to application.
