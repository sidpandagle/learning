When to Use AI (and When Not To)

The tasks where AI compounds your output — and the ones where it quietly wastes your time.

AI is a force multiplier — but only on the right tasks

People who get the most from AI don't use it for everything. They use it strategically — on a specific class of tasks where AI consistently adds value, and they skip it or use it carefully on tasks where it creates more problems than it solves.

The distinction is simple: AI is excellent at generation and poor at judgment. When your bottleneck is producing a first draft, exploring options, or transforming existing content — AI compounds your output. When your bottleneck is judgment, verification, or sensitive human interaction — AI either can't help or actively misleads you.

The core distinction AI bottleneck = generation. You bottleneck = judgment. When you need generation, reach for AI. When you need judgment, stay in the driver's seat and use AI as a thinking partner at most.

The AI sweet spot: generation tasks

These are tasks where the bottleneck is producing something — and where the quality bar is "good enough to work with," not "perfect on the first try."

  • First drafts — emails, reports, proposals, documentation
  • Brainstorming — ideas, angles, objections, alternatives
  • Summarising — long documents, meeting notes, research
  • Reformatting — prose to bullets, data to narrative, formal to casual
  • Explaining — complex topics simplified for a specific audience
  • Editing and rewriting — restructuring, tightening, changing tone
  • Research starting points — landscape scans, question generation, initial frameworks

In all of these, AI gets you to 70–80% quality in seconds. You then spend your time on the final 20% — the judgment, the polish, the nuance that actually requires your expertise.

The AI danger zone: judgment tasks

These are tasks where accuracy, trust, or consequences are high — and where AI's confidence is decoupled from its reliability.

  • Fact verification — checking specific claims, dates, citations, statistics
  • Final decisions — hiring, strategy calls, legal or financial choices
  • Emotional conversations — difficult feedback, sensitive negotiations, personal conflicts
  • Legal, medical, or compliance advice — AI is not a licensed professional
  • Real-time information — AI's training has a cutoff and it can't browse unless explicitly given tools
The hallucination problem — revisited AI models produce false information with genuine confidence. This is harmless in a brainstorm. It's a serious problem in a client-facing document. The danger zone tasks are where a confident hallucination causes real damage. Always verify before trusting AI on consequential specifics.

Most everyday tasks fall in the top row The majority of work tasks — drafting, summarising, reformatting, explaining — are generation tasks with moderate stakes. This is AI's strongest territory. If you're underusing AI, it's often because you're only trying it on the bottom-right quadrant.

⚠️

Your reflex: Over-reliant

You're reaching for AI in high-stakes or judgment-heavy situations without enough verification. The risk is confident errors slipping through unchecked.

🐢

Your reflex: Under-reliant

You're leaving value on the table by not reaching for AI on generation tasks where it would save significant time. The cost is doing manually what AI could draft in seconds.

🎯

Your reflex: Well-calibrated

You have a good instinct for when AI adds value and when it doesn't. The refinement is making this instinct more automatic, especially for edge cases.

Your calibration practice

For the next week, before using any AI output, ask: "If this is wrong, what happens?" If the answer is "something consequential," verify before using. Build a checklist habit for high-stakes outputs: every specific claim, date, and number gets confirmed from a primary source.

Your calibration practice

For the next week, any time you start a writing or drafting task, try AI first for 5 minutes before doing it yourself. You're not committing to using the output — just testing whether it gets you 70% of the way there faster than starting from scratch. Track your results.

Your refinement practice

Your instincts are sound. The next level is speed — making the triage automatic. Take the 2×2 matrix and mentally categorise the next 10 tasks that cross your desk. After two weeks, the classification will happen in under 2 seconds without thinking about it.

What you've learned

  • AI is strong at generation (drafting, brainstorming, summarising) and weak at judgment (decisions, verification, sensitive interactions)
  • The 2×2 triage: generation/judgment × low-stakes/high-stakes → four quadrants with different approaches
  • Most everyday work tasks fall in the top row — this is where AI compounds your output most reliably
  • The danger zone is high-stakes + judgment: AI can assist your thinking but can't own the outcome
